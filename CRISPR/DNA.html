WHAT IS DNA?

DNA or deoxyribonucleic acid is the molecule that determines an organism's characteristics.

In 1953 James Watson and Francis Crick successfully described its structure hence why the model used to describe DNA is called the Watson-Crick model. DNA is made up of nucleotides, which are made of 3 parts: phosphate, deoxyribose sugar, and the base (phosphate and deoxyribose sugar make up the outside/twisty bit)

All DNA is made up of the double helix shapes (twisted ladder). They contain nitrogen rich bases. There are 4 types: adenine(a), thymine(t), guanine(g), cytosine(c). Adenine(a) and thymine(t) can only go together, and guanine(g) and cytosine(c), can only go together. Therefore there can only be 2 types of rungs on a ladder, AT runs and CG rungs. Every now and again they won’t go together correctly and something like an adenine(a) and cytosine(c) may come together and it causes problems.

Chromosomes are long, thin and threadlike structures found in all cells with a nucleus. Each nucleus contains about 2m of tightly coiled DNA. Each cell (with a nucleus) contain 46 chromosomes in 23 pairs.

Gametes are the exception. Sperm and egg cells have 23 chromosomes (one of each pair).

Genomes (Genes) are sections of DNA. Each chromosome can have over 1000 genes. What makes one gene different from another is the order of AT and CG strands and it’s total length. The order of bases along the DNA strand is the genetic code. Each gene has code for a specific protein.

Proteins can be: structural, enzymes and regulatory (controlling things like growth hormones).

Each nucleus contains about 2m of tightly coiled DNA. Each cell (with a nucleus) contains 46 chromosomes in 23 pairs. Inside the Chromosomes, there are the DNA strands, and Genomes (Genes) are sections of that DNA.

     

HISTORY OF DNA

1869, Johannes Friedrich Miescher isolated a previously unknown chemical from the nuclei of dead white blood cells. Called this new chemical ‘nuclein’, eventually then called DNA. Phoebus Levene studied the nucleic acids. Identified the components of DNA and the arrangement of sugar, phosphate and base in a nucleotide. Though the DNA molecule was too simple to store the genetic code.

1943, Oswald Avery proved Levene wrong- DNA does hold genetic code.

1940s, Erwin Chargaff expanded on Levene’s work. Made three discoveries. - Nucleotides are not arranged in the same order in all species. - In all species, the amounts of adenine and thymine in the DNA are always similar, as are the amounts of guanine and cytosine. THis is known as Chargaff’s rule. - The amount of adenine plus guanine is always equal to the amount of thymine plus cytosine.
